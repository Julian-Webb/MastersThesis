\section{Preprocessing} \label{sec:preprocessing}

\subsection{Timestamp Localization} \label{subsec:timestamp-localization}
The \gls{edf} files---which contain the \gls{eeg} data---encode their recording onset as a naive timestamp, i.e., without any associated time zone information. The presence of \gls{dst} transitions caused timestamps within a single patient’s recordings to correspond to different UTC offsets.
To resolve this, all timestamps for a given patient were converted to that patient’s standard (\enquote{winter}) time based on their locale (Germany/United Kingdom).

A key difficulty in this procedure arises from the backward time shift in autumn, which generates ambiguous local times during the one-hour interval that occurs twice.
For such ambiguous timestamps, the correct UTC offset cannot be determined in isolation; it can only be inferred from an ordered sequence of timestamps in which the local time monotonically increases until a discontinuous one-hour backward jump occurs.

For the competition patients, this ordered sequence was constructed as follows: all files were first sorted by recording order, and then their start and end times were interleaved in the sequence \textit{file1 start, file1 end, file2 start, file2 end, …}.
This ordering enabled detection of points where the end time of the preceding file was chronologically later than the start time of the subsequent file, which is indicative of a backward time shift and thus marks the transition from \gls{dst} to standard time.
In turn, this made it possible to delineate which files were recorded under \gls{dst} and to apply the appropriate conversion to standard time.

For ULTRA2 patients, the sequence of recording was not known, but fortunately, the \gls{edf} files additionally contained explicit time zone metadata, which was used directly for the timestamp conversion.

\subsection{EEG Artifact Removal} \label{subsec:eeg-artifact-removal}
Two recurrent artifact morphologies were observed in the \gls{eeg} recordings.
These events exhibited extreme amplitudes relative to the physiological background and could have biased downstream processing (e.g., model training and cycle extraction), thus motivating their detection and removal.
As shown in \cref{fig:artifact-examples}, these artifacts typically occurred concurrently in both channels.
The affected samples reached the minimum representable amplitude of \SI{-1374.21}{\micro\volt}.

\begin{figure}[htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics{artifact_examples}
    \caption[Typical \gls{eeg} artifacts]{\textbf{Typical \gls{eeg} artifacts.}\\Excluded intervals are highlighted in red. Left: brief saturation (single-sample clipping). Right: prolonged artifact with clipping followed by high-frequency oscillations and \gls{dc} drift.}
    \label{fig:artifact-examples}
\end{figure}

In the first morphology, the signal saturates (\enquote{clips}) at the measurement limit for one or more samples (left panel of \cref{fig:artifact-examples}).
In the second morphology, the clipped segment is followed by rapid oscillations and a subsequent baseline shift (\gls{dc} drift) before returning to nominal activity (right panel of \cref{fig:artifact-examples}).

To detect these artifacts, all signals were first scanned for clipped intervals, defined as samples within \SI{0.1}{\micro\volt} of the saturation levels on either channel.
After the end of each clipped interval, the variance was computed in sliding windows of \SI{2}{\sec} with a step size of \SI{0.5}{\sec}.
Consecutive windows were excluded while their variance remained above a fixed threshold.
This high-variance criterion captured both the oscillatory component and the associated \gls{dc} drift.
Several variance thresholds were evaluated empirically; a threshold of \SI{20000}{\micro\volt\squared} reliably detected these artifacts without excessive rejection of valid data.
Threshold selection was constrained to avoid over-removal, because elevated variance also frequently occurs during periictal periods, which are central to the subsequent analysis.
Detected invalid intervals were symmetrically padded by \SI{0.25}{\sec} to remove potentially contaminated, adjacent samples.
Finally, invalid intervals separated by less than one segment's duration were merged, as the segment was the shortest analysis window used in this work.

This procedure yielded a set of \textbf{invalid recording intervals} that were excluded from subsequent analyses, and complementary \textbf{valid intervals} that were retained.


\subsection{Windowing and Labeling} \label{subsec:windowing}
For subsequent processing, the continuous \gls{eeg} recordings were represented on two aligned temporal grids: short \textit{segments} (used for feature extraction and model input) and longer \textit{clips} (used primarily for evaluation).
In addition, each segment was assigned a seizure-relative \textit{interval type}.
This section defines the interval types and then describes the construction and labeling of segments and clips.


\subsubsection{Interval types} \label{subsubsec:interval-types}
For seizure prediction to be clinically actionable, a minimum intervention interval is required between the time a warning is issued and seizure onset.
This interval provides time for countermeasures (e.g., administration of an inhibitory medication) or for the patient to take precautionary actions.

Moreover, a warning is only informative if seizure onset occurs within a predefined time horizon after the intervention interval.
This horizon is referred to as the \textit{\gls{sph}}.
Accordingly, the interval of equal duration that precedes the intervention interval---during which a warning should be raised---is labeled \textit{preictal} (\cref{fig:seizure-related-intervals}).

Periods far from any seizure are labeled \textit{interictal}.
In the vicinity of seizures (outside the preictal interval), \gls{eeg} dynamics may deviate from the interictal baseline and transitional phases are expected.
To capture these effects, additional periictal types are defined: the interval immediately preceding the preictal period (\textit{inter-pre}), the interval immediately following a seizure onset (\textit{postictal})\footnote{Due to the sparse availability of seizure offsets, seizures are treated as single time points in this work, which is why the postictal interval's onset corresponds to the seizures onset. Although seizures have a duration in reality, this is not crucial here, particularly because selected periictal intervals are excluded when necessary.}, and the subsequent recovery period (\textit{inter-post}) (\cref{fig:seizure-related-intervals}).

These interval definitions follow common conventions in the seizure prediction literature \parencite{brinkmann_crowdsourcing_2016, kuhlmann_epilepsyecosystemorg_2018, muller_coherent_2022}.

It is possible for seizures to occur in close succession, such that some of their \textbf{intervals overlap}.
As mentioned (\cref{sec:inclusion-criteria}), for a seizure to be valid, and thus eligible for further processing, it must have occurred at least $\sim$\SI{65}{\minute} after the previous seizure, so that its full preictal and intervention intervals did not contain the previous seizure.
This condition guaranteed that full preictal intervals were available for every seizure.
However, if a seizure occurred up to $\sim$\SI{8}{\hour} after a previous seizure, intervals still overlapped.
In this case, the latter seizure's preictal, intervention, postictal, and inter-pre intervals (partially) overwrote the prior seizure's postictal and inter-post intervals (\cref{fig:overlapping-periictal-intervals}).
This was done because it was the priority to correctly assign preictal---rather than post-seizure---intervals for conflicting interval types, to benefit training and evaluation.


To ensure that labeling boundaries coincide with the segment grid defined below, all interval durations were set to integer multiples of the segment duration (\cref{tab:interval-durations}).

\subsubsection{Segments}
Segments are the shortest analysis windows used in this work. 
Following \textcite{muller_coherent_2022, yang_seizure_2024}, their target duration was \SI{15}{\sec}.
However, the UNEEG recordings were sampled at an unusual frequency of \SI{207.0310546581987}{\hertz}, for which a \SI{15}{\sec} window would correspond to a fractional number of samples.
The segment duration was therefore adjusted to the nearest value yielding an integer sample count: \SI{14.99775}{\sec} (exactly \SI{3105}{samples}).

To create segments, the time span from the start of the first valid recording interval to the end of the last was partitioned into contiguous, non-overlapping segments, yielding a gap-free segment grid.
Segments fully contained within valid recording intervals were labeled as \textit{existing}; segments that were only partially contained (or lay outside) were treated as \textit{non-existing} and were excluded from subsequent analyses.

Each existing segment was assigned an \textbf{interval type} indicating its position relative to nearby seizures (\cref{fig:seizure-related-intervals}).
The type was determined based on the segment's \textit{end time}, reflecting that predictions could be issued at the end of a clip, which was aligned to segment endpoints.

\subsubsection{Clips}
Next, non-overlapping clips of approximately \SI{10}{\min} were constructed on the segment grid.
A clip typically spanned 40 consecutive segments, irrespective of whether individual segments were existing.
As above, the exact clip duration was chosen as an integer multiple of the segment duration (\cref{tab:interval-durations}).

The use of fixed-length clips followed prior work \parencite{brinkmann_crowdsourcing_2016, kuhlmann_epilepsyecosystemorg_2018, muller_coherent_2022}, but, in contrast to these studies, clips were not separated by \SI{10}{\sec} gaps, because the subsequent cycle extraction required continuous data.

Clips inherited their interval-type composition from the segments they contained and could therefore span multiple interval types.
Because clips were used for later evaluation, they should not mix preictal and non-preictal segments, as such clips could not have been assigned an unambiguous class label.
To prevent this, clips were anchored at preictal interval ends: one clip terminated at each preictal boundary (\enquote{seed}), and additional clips were generated by stepping backward on the segment grid\footnote{This anchoring strategy being applied to the test set was one of the reasons the evaluation cannot be considered purely pseudo-prospective in the sense of entirely forward-generated windows.} (\cref{fig:clip-generation}).
Consequently, each \textbf{preictal interval} was fully covered by exactly \textbf{six clips}, due to all interval durations being defined as multiples of segments.
In addition to preictal boundaries, the end of the last valid recording interval was used as a seed to generate clips for the recordings after the last recorded seizure.

Because the duration between segments containing seizures was usually not an integer multiple of the clip length, the number of segments between consecutive preictal interval ends was typically not divisible by 40.
Consequently, when generating clips backwards, the final clip before the next preictal interval usually contained fewer than 40 segments.

Thus, two clip-level \textbf{inclusion criteria} were applied.
First, shortened clips that did not contain the the full set of 40 segment slots (existing or not) were not considered structurally valid and therefore excluded.
Second, at least 35 of these segments had to be existing, ensuring that the clip lay predominantly within valid recording time.
This allowed short artifacts or minor edge truncation while excluding clips with substantial missing portions.
Clips failing either criterion were marked invalid and excluded from subsequent steps as appropriate.


\subsection{Dataset Partitioning} \label{subsec:dataset-partitioning}
In machine learning, models are trained on a subset of data and their performance evaluated on unseen data.
Accordingly, the data were partitioned into training and test sets.
Since predictive models must use past data to predict future events, the time series was split at a certain time point, rather than allocating individual clips to the sets.
This design also preserved long, uninterrupted recording intervals required for evaluating the multidien cycles.

For partitioning, the training set had to include enough examples of each class to learn predictive patterns, and the test set had to be large enough for accurate performance estimates and, for cycle evaluation, had to be at least as long as the longest cycle of 50 days, but optimally contain multiple such cycles.

The two classes considered here are preictal and non-preictal.
Because each seizure contributes only $\sim$\SI{1}{\hour} of preictal data and seizures typically occur only every few days or less, the resulting dataset exhibits a pronounced class imbalance (often 100:1 \parencite{brinkmann_crowdsourcing_2016}), characterized by a scarcity of preictal segments and an abundance of non-preictal segments.
Specifically, only \SI{0.76}{\percent} of clips across all patients were labeled as preictal.
Consequently, it was essential to account for the number of seizures, and thus preictal samples, per partition, in addition to the recording duration, when partitioning the dataset.
The objective was therefore to allocate approximately \SI{60}{\percent} of seizures to the training set and approximately \SI{40}{\percent} to the test set.
These proportions were based on \textcite{yang_seizure_2024} using \SI{60}{\percent} of data for training.

The partition boundary was determined per patient as a timestamp on the clip grid:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item 
    First, seizures were ordered chronologically and an \textit{initial split} was targeted at \textit{\SI{60}{\percent} training} seizures and \textit{\SI{40}{\percent} test} seizures.
    If $N$ denotes the number of valid seizures, the number initially allocated to training was $\lfloor 0.6N \rfloor$. The \textit{approximate split timestamp} was set to the \textit{midpoint} between the \textit{last training seizure} and the \textit{first test seizure}.
    \item 
    This timestamp was then aligned to the clip representation by \textit{snapping it to a clip start}.
    \item
    Next, the split was constrained using valid clips to ensure adequate \gls{eeg} data in both partitions: the training partition had to contain between \SI{40}{\percent} and \SI{70}{\percent} of valid clips.
    If the snapped split violated these bounds, the split position was shifted in clip-index space to the nearest admissible bound.
    \item
    Finally, the boundary was required to start at an \textit{interictal clip}; if necessary, the closest interictal clip was selected (searching forward or backward depending on which bound adjustment was needed).
    The start time of this clip was used as the final boundary.
\end{enumerate}

As previously mentioned, patient U03 had a large missing recording period in the middle of his monitoring timespan.
Following the partitioning procedure, the boundary would have fallen slightly before this gap, creating a very short portion of data, followed by a large gap.
This hindered the cycle evaluation; therefore, the boundary was manually nudged to fall into the gap for this patient.

The \textbf{spectral band powers} of each channel's signal, and \textbf{Pearson's correlation coefficient} between the two channels were extracted, because they were used in many of the top-performing algorithms in the competitions \parencite{brinkmann_crowdsourcing_2016, muller_coherent_2022, kuhlmann_epilepsyecosystemorg_2018}.
Additionally, the \textbf{signal variance} and \textbf{\gls{acfw}} were used as markers for \textit{critical slowing} \parencite{maturana_critical_2020}.
The exact \textit{numeric properties} of these features were essential for usage as model input and cycle extraction, and are therefore discussed below.

Note that single-channel features are subsequently denoted by their name, followed by the initial letter of the corresponding channel (\textbf{D}istal / \textbf{P}roximal), e.g., \enquote{ACFW (P)}.

\subsubsection{Variance}
The variance measures the average squared deviation of a signal $s$ from its mean $\bar{s}$ within a segment, and was computed \textit{for each segment and channel}.

\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:variance}
    \operatorname{Var}_s = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \left(s_t-\bar{s}\right)^2
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Pearson correlation coefficient}
Pearson's correlation coefficient (bounded in [\num{-1}, \num{1}]) was the only cross-channel feature used here.
It quantifies how the two channel signals $s$ and $r$ vary together within a segment and is defined as the normalized linear covariance:

\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:pearson-correlation}
    \rho_{sr} =
    \frac{1}{T}
    \frac{\sum_{t=1}^{T} \left(s_t-\bar{s}\right)\left(r_t-\bar{r}\right)}{\sqrt{\operatorname{Var}_s\,\operatorname{Var}_r}}
\end{equation}

where $\bar{s}$ and $\bar{r}$ are the signal means over the $T$ samples of the segment.

\subsubsection{Autocorrelation function width}
The \gls{acf} (bounded in [\num{-1}, \num{1}]) represents the correlation of a signal $s$ with itself shifted by a lag value $\lambda$, which corresponds to a number of samples:

\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:acf}
    \operatorname{ACF}_\lambda =
    \frac{1}{T} \frac    {\sum_{t=1}^{T-\lambda}\left(s_t-\bar{s}\right)\left(s_{t+\lambda}-\bar{s}\right)}{\operatorname{Var}_s}
\end{equation}

where $\bar{s}$ is the signal mean, and $T$ is the number of total samples.

The \textbf{\gls{acfw}} is then defined as the \textbf{lag $\lambda$} where the \gls{acf} reaches its half-maximum value of $0.5$ and can thus range from 1 to 3105 (the number of samples in a segment).
It can be interpreted as a measure of temporal persistence: it quantifies how quickly a signal decorrelates over time.
This measure was calculated \textit{for each segment and channel}.

\subsubsection{Spectral band powers}
{\sisetup{range-phrase = {--}, range-units = single}
Lastly, the spectral band powers were extracted for the delta (\SIrange{0.5}{4}{\hertz}), theta (\SIrange{4}{8}{\hertz}), alpha (\SIrange{8}{12}{\hertz}), beta (\SIrange{12}{35}{\hertz}), and gamma (\SIrange{35}{100}{\hertz}) bands.
They were computed \textit{for each segment and channel} as follows:}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The \gls{psd} was estimated using Welch’s method by splitting the signal into Hann-windowed subwindows of length $2/f_{\min}$ (with $f_{\min}=\SI{0.5}{\Hz}$), using \SI{50}{\percent} overlap, detrending by subtracting the mean, and density scaling (unit $\si{\volt}^2 / r\si{\Hz}$).
    \item For each band, the (absolute) band power was obtained by numerically integrating the \gls{psd} between the band limits using Simpson’s rule, with the frequency-bin spacing as the integration step.
    \item The absolute (rather than relative) band powers were used to maintain comparability between segments.
\end{enumerate}

The result was one scalar band-power value \textit{per band and per channel} (ten values total for two channels and five bands).
