On the \textbf{train set}, the models achieved a high \gls{roc auc} (\SIrange{66}{97}{\percent}, mean: \SI{83}{\percent}) and \gls{eb} score (\SIrange{64}{98}{\percent}, mean: \SI{81}{\percent}), i.e.,  high \gls{eb} sensitivity and \gls{eb} specificity (low relative \gls{tifw}).

On the \textbf{test set}, model performance decreased or remained stable in most cases  (mean \gls{roc auc}: \SI{67}{\percent}, mean \gls{eb} score: \SI{67}{\percent}).
The classifiers did not perform better than chance on the test set for patient U03's ensemble and both models of U04, as measured by the Hanley McNeil test.

For the majority of patients and metrics, the \gls{cnn} performed better than the ensemble.
For the training set, the \gls{cnn} performed significantly better for relative \gls{tifw} (\gls{eb} specificity), \gls{eb} score, and \gls{roc auc}, but not for \gls{eb} sensitivity, as measured by the Wilcoxon test (\cref{tab:model-comparison}).
On the test set, while performance is much more similar, as measured by the mean per metric across patients (\cref{tab:model_eval_metrics}), the \gls{cnn} still tended to perform better.
However, for the test set, no model showed statistically significantly higher performance than the other model.

Although the \gls{cnn} still performed better than the ensemble on the test set, the mean \textit{percentage-point decline} in performance from the train to test sets of the \gls{cnn} vs.\ ensemble was higher for \gls{roc auc} (22 vs.\ 12), \gls{eb} specificity (19 vs.\ 9), \gls{eb} sensitivity (16 vs.\ 12), and \gls{eb} score (17 vs.\ 10).

\section{Co-Occurrence of Prediction Errors}\label{sec:results-co-occurrence-of-prediction-errors}
The \glspl{cope} for patients and partitions are shown in \cref{fig:copes} and \cref{tab:copes}.
A strong correlation ($>0.5$) was found for most combinations, except for U04-test and for U12 in both partitions.
Even in these cases, the correlation was substantially positive.
In many cases, the \gls{cope} was even above \num{0.8}, indicating a very strong correlation of \glspl{fp}.
Unlike model performance, often remained stable, and sometimes even increased, rather than always declining, when moving from the train to test set.

In some cases, the \gls{cope} was lower where models performed poorly, such as for patients U03-test and U04-test, which were also the cases where models were not shown to perform better than chance.
However, the same could not be said about their training sets, since models performed acceptably (\gls{roc auc}$\approx$\SI{66}{\percent}) to very well (\gls{roc auc}$\approx$\SI{90}{\percent}) there.
However, patient U12 showed contrary findings: here, models performed quite well, especially for the training set, yet this patient had the lowest \gls{cope} scores.

To investigate whether \gls{cope} between the two classifiers was indeed associated with model performance, a multiple linear regression analysis was conducted. The dependent target variable was the Fisher's z-transformed \gls{cope}, as a measure to achieve a normal distribution and stabilize variance. The independent variables were the patient-wise difference between the classifiers’ \glspl{roc auc} after logit transformation, as well as their average \gls{roc auc} score. 

Although the regression model explained approximately 42.8\% of the variance in the transformed \gls{cope} (\(R^2 = 0.428\)), it did not reach statistical significance (\(p = 0.081\)). This is likely attributable to the small sample size used in the present analysis. The average model performance was positively associated with higher \gls{cope} (\(\beta = 0.328\), \(p = 0.070\)), indicating a strong statistical trend. In contrast, a larger performance difference between the two classifiers was negatively associated with \gls{cope} (\(\beta = -0.468\), \(p = 0.245\)).
