\section{Model Evaluation and Comparison} \label{sec:model-eval-and-comparison}
\Cref{tab:model_eval_metrics} shows the resulting evaluation metrics from \cref{sec:model-evaluation}.
For the training set, we generally see high a \gls{roc auc} (\SIrange{66}{97}{\percent}, mean: \SI{83}{\percent}) and \gls{eb} score (\SIrange{64}{98}{\percent}, mean: \SI{81}{\percent}), i.e., low relative \gls{tifw} and high \gls{eb} sensitivity.
For the test set, the models perform worse or equally in almost all cases.
% todo for discussion:
% This can be either because of overfitting or because the distribution of the test set changes.
The classifiers do not perform better than chance on the test set for patient U03's ensemble and both models of U04, as indicated by the Hanley McNeil p-values $\geq$ \SI{5}{\percent},

For the majority of patients and metrics, the \gls{cnn} performed better than the ensemble.
This is consistent with \textcite{muller_coherent_2022} who expected the ensemble to perform worse if the best features were not retrospectively selected from the test set, as is the case here.
