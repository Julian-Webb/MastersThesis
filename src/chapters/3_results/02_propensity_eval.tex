\section{Propensity Evaluation}\label{sec:propensity-evaluation}

\subsection{Statistical Results} \label{subsec:statistical-results}
From the twelve included patients, two models per patient, and 15 features, there are a total of 360 patient-model-feature combinations.
\Cref{tab:model-feature-qualifications} shows the combinations for which the criteria from \cref{sec:propensity-eval} hold, which are only a total of three (\SI{0.83}{\percent}).
For the remaining 357 combinations (\SI{99.27}{\percent}), the assumptions did not hold, which means either the seizures or \glspl{fp} were not phase locked to the features, the model's performance was too poor, or the phase distributions of seizures and \glspl{fp} were not similar enough.

\subsection{Descriptive Results} \label{subsec:descriptive-results}
Even though only very few patient-model-feature combinations meet the criteria, we can see that visually see that there is an effect for patient U01.
His seizures have a clear preferred phase (\cref{fig:U01-seizure-phases}).
Likewise, his \glspl{fp} have a clear preferred phase for both the \gls{cnn} (\cref{fig:U01-CNN-FP-phases}) and ensemble (\cref{fig:U01-ensemble-FP-phases}).
However, between seizures and \glspl{fp}, there is a phase delay, i.e., \glspl{fp} tend to occur somewhat later than seizures.
This effect is only pronounced for patient U03.

For other patients, this is not the case.
Patient C03 acts as an example here for when seizures are only phase locked to some or no features (\cref{fig:C03-seizure-phases}) and \glspl{fp} are not phase locked to features (\cref{fig:C03-CNN-FP-phases}, \cref{fig:C03-ensemble-FP-phases}).
This is what the phase locking more typically looks like for all patients, except U01.
